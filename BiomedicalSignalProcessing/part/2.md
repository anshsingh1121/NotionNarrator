# üß† 1. What is an Excitable Cell?

Excitable cells (like **neurons** and **muscle cells**) can **generate electrical signals**.
These electrical signals arise because there is a **difference in charge** between the inside and outside of the cell membrane.

Think of the cell membrane as a **semi-permeable wall**:

* Some ions (like K‚Å∫, Na‚Å∫, Cl‚Åª) can move across it easily.
* Others are blocked or move slowly.
* This uneven movement creates **electrical potential** ‚Äî like a battery!

---

# ‚ö° 2. The Concept of Membrane Potential

The **membrane potential** is the **voltage difference** between the inside and outside of a cell.

* Inside the cell ‚Üí usually **negative** (‚âà -70 mV)
* Outside the cell ‚Üí **positive**

This potential exists because:

* **Ions** (Na‚Å∫, K‚Å∫, Cl‚Åª) are **unevenly distributed**.
* The membrane is **selectively permeable** (mainly to K‚Å∫).
* **Ion pumps** (like Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase) maintain these gradients.

# ‚öôÔ∏è 4. The Nernst Equation ‚Äî One Ion at a Time

The **Nernst potential** gives the equilibrium voltage for *a single ion* ‚Äî when its diffusion (chemical force) balances its electrical force.

---

## üîπ Formula

[
E = \frac{(RT)}{zF} \ln\left(\frac{[C]*{out}}{[C]*{in}}\right)
]

At body temperature (37¬∞C ‚âà 310 K),
the constant ( \frac{RT}{F} ) ‚âà 26.7 mV,
and using base 10 logarithm:

[
E = 60 , \text{mV} \times \frac{1}{z} \log_{10}\left(\frac{[C]*{out}}{[C]*{in}}\right)
]

---

## üî∏ Example 1: Potassium (K‚Å∫)

[
E_K = 60 \log_{10}\left(\frac{5}{140}\right)
]

[
E_K = 60 \times \log_{10}(0.036) = 60 \times (-1.44) = -86 , \text{mV}
]

üëâ So the **equilibrium potential for K‚Å∫** is about **‚Äì86 mV**.

That means:
If the membrane potential = -86 mV, K‚Å∫ movement stops (forces are balanced).

---

## üî∏ Example 2: Sodium (Na‚Å∫)

[
E_{Na} = 60 \log_{10}\left(\frac{150}{15}\right)
]

[
E_{Na} = 60 \times \log_{10}(10) = 60 \times 1 = +60 , \text{mV}
]

üëâ So Na‚Å∫ would stop moving at **+60 mV**.

At rest (‚Äì70 mV), Na‚Å∫ wants to **enter** the cell strongly.

---

## üî∏ Example 3: Chloride (Cl‚Åª)

For negative ions, the formula inverts ([in]/[out]):

[
E_{Cl} = -60 \log_{10}\left(\frac{[Cl^-]*{out}}{[Cl^-]*{in}}\right)
]

[
E_{Cl} = -60 \log_{10}\left(\frac{120}{10}\right) = -60 \times 1.08 = -65 , \text{mV}
]

üëâ So Cl‚Åª equilibrium potential ‚âà **‚Äì65 mV**.

# üß© 5. The Goldman‚ÄìHodgkin‚ÄìKatz (GHK) Equation ‚Äî Combining All Ions

In real cells, more than one ion matters.
The **GHK equation** gives the *net* resting membrane potential (RMP), considering:

* The **permeability (P)** of each ion
* Their **concentrations**

---

## üîπ Simplified Equation

[
V_{rest} = 60 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_K[K^+]*{out} + P*{Na}[Na^+]*{out} + P*{Cl}[Cl^-]*{in}}{P_K[K^+]*{in} + P_{Na}[Na^+]*{in} + P*{Cl}[Cl^-]_{out}}\right)
]

*(Notice that for Cl‚Åª, inside/outside are flipped.)*

---

## üî∏ Example with Typical Values

Let:

* ( P_K = 1.0 )
* ( P_{Na} = 0.03 )
* ( P_{Cl} = 0.1 )

Concentrations:

* ( [K^+]*{out} = 5, [K^+]*{in} = 140 )
* ( [Na^+]*{out} = 150, [Na^+]*{in} = 15 )
* ( [Cl^-]*{out} = 120, [Cl^-]*{in} = 10 )

Now plug in:

**Numerator:**
[
(1)(5) + (0.03)(150) + (0.1)(10) = 5 + 4.5 + 1 = 10.5
]

**Denominator:**
[
(1)(140) + (0.03)(15) + (0.1)(120) = 140 + 0.45 + 12 = 152.45
]

[
V_{rest} = 60 \log_{10}\left(\frac{10.5}{152.45}\right)
]

[
V_{rest} = 60 \times \log_{10}(0.0689) = 60 \times (-1.162) = -69.7 , \text{mV}
]

‚úÖ **Resting membrane potential ‚âà ‚Äì70 mV**

This matches real neurons!

# ‚öñÔ∏è 6. Intuitive Picture

| Ion | Wants to move         | Force Direction at Rest (-70 mV)         | Effect                                      |
| --- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| K‚Å∫  | Outward (high inside) | Electrical pulls in, chemical pushes out | Small net outflow                           |
| Na‚Å∫ | Inward (high outside) | Both forces pull in                      | Strong inflow (but membrane less permeable) |
| Cl‚Åª | Inward                | Forces roughly balanced                  | Stable                                      |

The **balance of permeability** (mainly K‚Å∫) and concentration gradients creates the stable resting potential.

# üîã 7. Analogy: Cell as a Leaky Battery

Imagine:

* A **battery** (ion gradients) is charged by a **pump** (Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase).
* The **membrane** has tiny **holes (channels)** for K‚Å∫, Na‚Å∫, and Cl‚Åª.
* K‚Å∫ holes are wide open ‚Üí lots of K‚Å∫ leaks out ‚Üí inside becomes negative.
* A few Na‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª leaks modify the voltage to about **‚Äì70 mV**.
